## **Post 2 Extract:**
Measurement is the first thing generative AI broke, and most CMOs haven't admitted it yet.

For decades we lived and died by rankings and traffic. That worked when a user saw a list and picked a link. But AI Search doesn't return a list. The query fans out, the model synthesizes one answer, and ranking #1 stops mattering if you're not the citation the model trusts. 

Classic search was a performance channel. AI Search behaves more like a brand channel, so you have to think about performance differently.

The mistake I see executives make: they watch classic rankings dip, assume the team is failing, and miss that brand influence is actually climbing. Wrong scoreboard.

The teams winning the next 24 months are the ones rebuilding the scoreboard before the old one goes fully dark. The ones who don't will keep optimizing for a channel that's quietly collapsing and call the silence stability.

Brendan Hufford and I are getting into all of it live from Cannes Lions for the @Semrush CMO webinar series. Thursday, June 25 — 11 AM EDT / 5 PM CEST / 8 AM PDT.

## **Post 4 Extract:**
Cloaking has been a dirty word in SEO for 20+ years.

For good reason.

If you’re showing search engines one thing and users another to manipulate rankings, you’re asking to get smoked.

But AI crawlers are not search engines.

They don’t follow the same rules. They don’t render like Googlebot. They don’t have the same value exchange. And in many cases, they’re not sending you meaningful traffic while they’re happily consuming the original research, data, and frameworks you spent real money creating.

So the question becomes:

Why are we treating every AI crawler like it deserves the same access as Google?

I’m not saying you should hide everything. That would be stupid.

If you want to be cited in AI Overviews, ChatGPT, Perplexity, or whatever answer engine is next, you need to make the right content accessible.

But your moat? Your proprietary benchmarks? Your hard-won datasets? Your internal research? Your original frameworks?

That’s a different conversation.

The web was built around an implicit bargain:

You crawl my content. You send me traffic.

AI has broken that bargain.

So technical SEO needs to evolve from “make everything crawlable” to “make the right things accessible to the right systems for the right reasons.”

That means thinking at the edge. Thinking about rendering. Thinking about user agents. Thinking about markdown (purely for speed purposes). Thinking about what AI systems can and cannot actually see.

And, yes, thinking about cloaking differently.

I wrote up the technical case here:

https://lnkd.in/eRdRp9PS
